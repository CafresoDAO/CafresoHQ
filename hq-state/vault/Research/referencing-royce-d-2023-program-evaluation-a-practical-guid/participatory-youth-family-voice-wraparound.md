# Participatory Evaluation: Centering Youth and Family Voice in Wraparound Outcome Measurement

## Core Concept

Participatory evaluation positions youth and families not as passive subjects but as co-investigators — shaping what gets measured, how, and what the findings mean. For high-fidelity wraparound (HFW), this is especially important because wraparound is *by definition* family-driven and youth-guided. An evaluation that sidelines family voice contradicts the model's own philosophy.

## Royce (2023) Framework Connections

**Chapter 6 — Selecting an Evaluation Design:** Royce emphasizes that design choices must align with program philosophy. For wraparound, a design that excludes stakeholder voice risks construct invalidity — measuring outcomes the family never identified as meaningful. Participatory designs correct this by embedding voice into the design phase, not just data collection.

**Chapter 8 — Qualitative Approaches to Evaluation:** Royce discusses focus groups, in-depth interviews, and narrative methods as tools for capturing lived experience. Applied to wraparound, these methods allow youth and families to articulate *what changed*, *why it changed*, and *whether it mattered to them* — the three questions fidelity instruments (e.g., the Wraparound Fidelity Index / WFI) alone cannot answer.

**Chapter 11 — Collecting Data / Involving Stakeholders:** Royce calls out stakeholder involvement as both an ethical obligation and a quality-enhancing strategy. In wraparound evaluation, this maps to structured participatory methods: youth-led interviews, parent advisory review of draft findings, or co-interpretation sessions where families annotate data with their own meaning.

## Evidence from the Literature

- **YAP Ireland wraparound model** (Tandfonline, 2024): Used qualitative interviews and focus groups with young people, parents, advocates, and staff. Found that *how* youth experienced participation (voice, respect, genuine agency) was more predictive of sustained engagement than service volume metrics.
- **Youth Participatory Action Research (YPAR)** (ResearchGate, 2019): Semi-structured interviews with 13 stakeholders showed that youth-generated data surfaces program blind spots invisible to staff-only evaluations.
- **Better Evaluation — Participatory Evaluation Framework**: Recommends individual storytelling, participatory social mapping, and causal-linkage exercises — all compatible with wraparound's "natural supports" and "strengths-based" pillars.

## Application to HFW Outcome Evaluation

| Participatory Method | Wraparound Phase | What It Captures |
|---|---|---|
| Youth narrative interviews | 6-month & 12-month follow-up | Self-reported change in functioning, hope, belonging |
| Family focus groups | Transition-out planning | Family-identified sustainability factors |
| Causal-linkage mapping | Mid-implementation | Family's own theory of what's working and why |
| Photo-voice / digital storytelling | Any phase | Non-verbal / low-literacy youth perspectives |

## Key Tension to Acknowledge

Royce (Ch. 6 & 8) notes that participatory designs can introduce **bias toward socially desirable responses** when families feel the evaluator is connected to their service provider. HFW programs should use independent interviewers or peer-researchers (other families with lived experience) to preserve candor.
