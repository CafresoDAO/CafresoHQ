# Qualitative Outcome Measurement in High Fidelity Wraparound: Foundations

## Connecting Royce (2023) to Wraparound Evaluation

Royce (2023) *Program Evaluation: A Practical Guide* provides the methodological scaffolding for understanding how qualitative measurement contributes to outcome evaluation. Relevant chapters include:

- **Chapter 6 (Qualitative Approaches to Evaluation)** — Royce distinguishes qualitative methods (interviews, focus groups, observation) as essential when outcomes involve lived experience, relationship quality, and process fidelity — precisely the domains that matter most in wraparound.
- **Chapter 9 (Outcome Evaluation)** — Royce defines outcome evaluation as assessing whether a program achieves its intended changes in clients. For youth in High Fidelity Wraparound (HFW), long-term outcomes include placement stability, school engagement, behavioral health improvement, and family functioning.
- **Chapter 4 (Logic Models and Program Theory)** — Royce emphasizes linking inputs/activities to short-, intermediate-, and long-term outcomes. The California Wraparound Theory of Change maps directly to this framework, specifying qualitative and quantitative outcome measures across timeframes.

## What the Evidence Base Shows

- The **Wraparound Fidelity Assessment System (WFAS)** — including the Wraparound Fidelity Index (WFI), Team Observation Measure (TOM), and Document Assessment Review Tool (DART) — operationalizes fidelity measurement through both structured observation (qualitative) and record review.
- Qualitative measures in HFW capture **youth and family satisfaction**, voice and choice in planning, and cultural responsiveness — domains that quantitative metrics alone cannot adequately assess.
- A PubMed-indexed study (2018) on whether HFW saves money long-term notes the need for rigorous designs to assess causal impact; qualitative data is critical for understanding *why* outcomes occur, not just *whether* they do (aligns with Royce Ch. 9's discussion of process vs. outcome evaluation).
- California Wraparound Standards (Standard 10.2) require programs to evaluate effectiveness using both fidelity tools and qualitative outcome data including satisfaction and engagement.

## Key Qualitative Methods Applicable to HFW Evaluation (per Royce Ch. 6)

| Method | HFW Application |
|--------|----------------|
| Semi-structured interviews | Youth/family voice on goal progress, team helpfulness |
| Focus groups | Community partner perspectives on service coordination |
| Case record review (DART) | Document quality of care plans, meeting fidelity |
| Participant observation (TOM) | Direct observation of wraparound team meetings |
| Narrative inquiry | Family stories of change over time |

## Long-Term Outcome Domains for HFW

Per California Wraparound Theory of Change, long-term outcomes include:
- Sustained family stability and reduced child welfare involvement
- Improved behavioral health and reduced restrictive placements
- Youth achieving normative developmental milestones (graduation, employment)
- Family empowerment and natural support network growth

Royce (Ch. 9) would categorize these as **distal outcomes** — hardest to attribute but most meaningful for demonstrating program impact.
