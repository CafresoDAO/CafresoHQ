# Qualitative Outcome Measurement for High-Fidelity Wraparound Services

## Grounding in Royce (2023)

Royce's *Program Evaluation: A Practical Guide for Social Work and the Helping Professions* (1st ed., Cognella, 2023) dedicates substantial attention to qualitative and mixed-methods approaches as legitimate, rigorous tools for outcome evaluation — not just process description. Key chapters:

- **Chapter 4 – Qualitative and Mixed Methods in Evaluation**: Royce presents qualitative methods as essential when outcomes are complex, individualized, or not reducible to numeric scales. He outlines approaches including in-depth interviews, focus groups, narrative inquiry, and case studies. For populations like youth and families in wraparound, where outcomes are multidimensional (safety, stability, family functioning, community connection), qualitative methods can surface meaning that standardized instruments miss.

- **Chapter 5 – Formative and Process Evaluation**: Royce distinguishes between process fidelity and outcome quality. High-fidelity wraparound requires both: fidelity monitoring (Is the model being implemented as designed?) and outcome tracking (Are families achieving their individualized goals?). Qualitative methods serve both layers — documenting implementation quality and capturing family-defined success.

- **Chapter 7 – Client Satisfaction**: Royce treats satisfaction data as a proximal outcome indicator but cautions it is insufficient alone for long-term outcome claims. For wraparound, satisfaction surveys should be paired with qualitative interviews to distinguish felt sense of support from durable change.

## Why Qualitative Matters for Wraparound Outcome Evaluation

High-fidelity wraparound (as defined by Burns et al., 2000) is **individualized by design** — each plan is unique to the child and family. This creates a measurement challenge: standardized instruments may not capture the specific outcomes a family set for themselves. Royce's framework supports using **goal attainment scaling (GAS)** combined with narrative interviews as a qualitatively-sensitive outcome approach.

Key qualitative methods Royce endorses for outcome evaluation:
- **In-depth interviews** with youth and caregivers at multiple time points (baseline, midpoint, exit, follow-up)
- **Focus groups** with natural supports and community partners to assess systemic change
- **Case study analysis** to trace causal pathways between wraparound activities and long-term outcomes
- **Document review** of wraparound plans over time to track goal evolution and attainment

## Fidelity as a Mediator of Long-Term Outcomes

Research on wraparound (Bruns, Suter & Leverentz-Brady, 2008; PMC3086808) identifies the **Wraparound Fidelity Index (WFI)** as the most common fidelity instrument. Royce's Chapter 5 framework supports treating fidelity as a **mediating variable** in outcome evaluation: higher fidelity → stronger alliance and individualization → better long-term outcomes.

A qualitative evaluation design aligned with Royce would:
1. Assess implementation fidelity (WFI + facilitator interviews)
2. Track family-defined outcomes qualitatively (narrative interviews)
3. Use mixed methods to connect fidelity scores to narrative outcome themes

## Long-Term Outcome Considerations

Royce (Chapter 4) notes that qualitative longitudinal designs are particularly suited to capturing **sustained change** — the kind wraparound aims to produce (reduced out-of-home placement, improved school stability, family reunification). Short-term satisfaction data does not address whether gains persist. Recommended: follow-up interviews at 6 and 12 months post-exit.
