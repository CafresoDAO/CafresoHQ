# 03: Symptoms and Clinical Progression of Andes Virus

Following the [[01-initial-outbreak-summary|initial outbreak]], understanding the clinical presentation of the Andes virus is critical. The virus causes a severe respiratory illness known as Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome (HPS), sometimes called Hantavirus Cardiopulmonary Syndrome (HCPS). The disease progression typically occurs within 1 to 8 weeks after exposure.

The clinical course of HPS is generally divided into three phases:

1.  **Prodromal Phase:** This initial phase presents with non-specific, flu-like symptoms. Patients commonly experience fever, muscle aches (myalgia), headaches, and fatigue.
2.  **Cardiopulmonary Phase:** This is the most severe stage. Patients rapidly develop respiratory distress, including coughing and shortness of breath (dyspnea) as their lungs fill with fluid (pulmonary edema). This can quickly lead to shock and cardiac complications.
3.  **Recovery Phase:** If the patient survives the cardiopulmonary phase, a gradual recovery period follows.

Key laboratory findings often include thrombocytopenia (low platelet count), leukocytosis (elevated white blood cell count), and hemoconcentration. These markers are crucial for diagnosis, especially given the non-specific early symptoms and the known risk of [[02-andes-virus-transmission|person-to-person transmission]].
